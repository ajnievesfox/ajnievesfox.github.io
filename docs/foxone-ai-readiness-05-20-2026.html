This brief, how Google's guide to AI search optimization aligns to current SEO work, and our positioning on the new agentic ceiling.

Preface

Google's public guidance isn't the full picture

A 2024 leak of internal API documentation, later acknowledged in DOJ antitrust proceedings, surfaced signals Google has historically downplayed: site-level authority scoring, click-behavior re-ranking, sandbox-like behavior for new domains, and LLM-driven content quality measurement. The May 2026 guide is useful, but we need to be mindful that it reflects what Google wants publishers to focus on for Google, not the universal rulebook for every AI system.

Agentic readiness, the new ceiling

Google's product team disagrees with Google's guidance team

Four days after the AI Optimization Guide, Google announced Search Agents.

Google introduced Search Agents at I/O 2026 on May 19. AI agents that run in the background, scanning blogs, news, social posts, and real-time data on finance, shopping, and sports to surface updates for subscribed users. Google's own launch example: "the instant any of your favorite pro athletes announce a sneaker collab." Search Agents launch first to AI Pro and Ultra subscribers this summer.

This is a new publisher visibility surface the May 15 guide does not address. The data Search Agents read is structured, time-bound, and fresh. That is exactly what FOX One's event pages, schema layer, and unified schedule pipeline already produce. The gap to close is making sure that data is exposed in formats agents can consume.

Google's own tooling now scores agent readiness

Chrome added a measurable Agentic Browsing category to Lighthouse on May 5.

Two weeks before the AI Optimization Guide published, Chrome added a new Agentic Browsing audit category to Lighthouse, the same tool that scores Performance, Accessibility, and SEO. It evaluates four signals:

  • WebMCP. An API that exposes a site's logic and forms to AI agents (declarative HTML or imperative JS).
  • Agent-centric accessibility. Names, labels, tree integrity, and visibility in the accessibility tree, which is the machine-eye view of a page.
  • Layout stability (CLS). Agents fail when elements move between identification and interaction.
  • llms.txt. A machine-readable site summary at the domain root.

None of these signals appear in the May 15 guide. All four appear in Google's official audit tool. This is the operational definition of the agentic ceiling. Written by Google, for Google's own browser, before Google's public guidance acknowledged any of it.

The architecture behind AI Mode has been in Google's patents since 2018

A single AI Mode query triggers 5–20 sub-queries, each retrieving and grading sources independently

Five patents map directly to the agentic RAG architecture running inside AI Mode today:

  • Query decomposition and fan-out (US11663201B2, filed 2018)
  • Routing among retrieval sources (US20240362093A1, 2024)
  • Stateful chat memory (US20240289407A1, 2024)
  • Pairwise passage ranking (US20250124067A1, 2024)
  • Generative answer synthesis (US11769017B1, 2023)

What this means operationally: Indexing is the floor, not the win. You also need broad topic coverage, self-contained passages that beat competitors in pairwise ranking, and freshness signals the system can verify.

Considerable Contradictions

Microsoft published their AI search optimization guide in October 2025. Google's guide, published seven months later, explicitly debunks several practices Microsoft recommends.

Content chunking for AI


Explicitly debunked. Google says there is no need to break content into small pieces; its systems understand nuance across full pages. Listed as a myth in the May 2026 guide.


Core to their framework. Copilot and Bing-powered assistants parse content into modular pieces for ranking and assembly into answers. Microsoft encourages chunk-friendly formatting (headings, Q&A blocks, lists).


Continue to recommend clear heading hierarchy and semantic structure for human readability. That satisfies Microsoft's parsing model without the artificial chunking Google debunks.

Schema markup for AI features


Schema is not required for generative AI search, and there is no special markup to add. Google still recommends structured data for rich results, but not as an AI-specific lever.


Schema is a key recommendation: it turns plain text into structured data machines interpret with confidence. Positioned as a primary optimization lever for Copilot and Bing.


Continue implementing template-level JSON-LD for rich results and Bing/Copilot value. Do not add schema as an "AI hack"; scope it as sound technical SEO, which is how FOX One SEO has already framed it.

Rewriting content for AI systems


No need to write in a specific way for AI. Google's systems understand synonyms and general meaning; no AI-specific rewrites required.


Detailed writing-style guidance: avoid em dashes, keep punctuation simple, and write self-contained "snippable" sentences tuned for machine parsing.


Continue to recommend content optimizations that match search intent: keyword, SERP, and competitive analysis to target the phrases we want to rank for in traditional and AI search, then shaping content to match informational, transactional, or commercial intent. That is writing for the user, not for AI systems.
